Conventional methods to determine Boltzmann's constant $k$ make use of black-body radiation and its two famous laws — Wien's displacement law and Stefan's law. These methods are time-consuming and often lead to comparatively less accurate results.

The $V$–$I$ characteristics of a semiconductor diode can be used to determine Boltzmann's constant accurately and with simple equipment that can be handled with ease and convenience.

## Principle

The diode equation is:

$$ I = I_0 \left\{ \exp\!\left(\frac{eV}{\eta k T}\right) - 1 \right\} $$

where:

- $V$ — voltage across the diode
- $I$ — forward current at voltage $V$
- $I_0$ — reverse saturation current
- $k$ — Boltzmann's constant
- $T$ — temperature in Kelvin
- $\eta$ — a constant characteristic of the diode material; $\eta = 1$ for Ge and $\eta = 2$ for Si

If $V \gg kT/e$, Boltzmann's constant can be expressed as:

$$ k = \frac{e}{\eta T \left(\dfrac{\Delta \ln I}{\Delta V}\right)} $$

where $\dfrac{\Delta \ln I}{\Delta V}$ is the slope of the straight line drawn between $V$ and $\ln(I)$.
