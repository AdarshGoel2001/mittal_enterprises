## Objective

- Study the hysteresis curves of transformer stampings, ferrites and other magnetic materials of different shapes, and determine their energy losses.
- Study the hysteresis curve as a function of the magnetic field.
- Determine saturation magnetisation, remanence and coercivity of magnetic materials.

## Theory

In conventional techniques for tracing the B-H loop, one has to wind primary and secondary coils on the specimen — not convenient for quickly studying loops of different materials. The present technique traces the B-H loop by simply inserting the specimen in a magnetizing coil. It uses a specially designed integrated-circuit probe to measure the flux density $B$.

The current flowing through the magnetizing coil develops a potential difference across the resistance $R$, which deflects the CRO beam in the X-direction. The deflection is proportional to the magnetic field $H$:

$$ H = \frac{N \cdot V_X}{R \cdot L} \quad \text{ampere-turns/meter} \quad \ldots (1) $$

where $N$ is the number of turns of the coil, $R$ is the resistance in series with the coil (between terminals G and H), $L$ is the coil length in metres, and $V_X$ is the voltage applied to the X-input of the CRO.

The probe has a sensitivity of 5 mV per Gauss, so:

$$ B = 0.5\,V_Y \quad \text{weber/m}^2 \quad \ldots (2) $$

where $V_Y$ is the voltage applied to the Y-input of the CRO.

## Instrument

The Universal B-H Curve Tracer consists of:

- **Main unit** — variable AC supply (marked $V_{a.c.}$), a resistor $R$ in series with a potentiometer ($P$), and input terminals for the CRO.
- **Probe unit** — IC probe with associated circuitry and one magnetizing coil (300 turns of SWG-26, coil length 0.0323 m).
- **Samples** — 5″ nail, ferrite rod and transformer stampings.
